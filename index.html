01 / Overview

Executive Summary

The Federal Open Market Committee voted 10-1 on March 18, 2026 to hold the federal funds rate at 3.50–3.75%, marking the third consecutive meeting without a rate change. Governor Stephen Miran cast the lone dissent, preferring a 25bp cut.

The statement acknowledged that "economic activity has been expanding at a solid pace" but noted that "job gains have remained low" and "inflation remains somewhat elevated." For the first time in this cycle, the Committee flagged "developments in the Middle East" as a source of elevated uncertainty.

The macro backdrop tells a more troubling story than the statement's measured language suggests. Core PCE has re-accelerated to 3.06% YoY, GDP growth collapsed to 0.7% in Q4 2025, and the Committee's own risk assessments reveal near-unanimous stagflation anxiety. This report examines the data the Committee faced and what their projections reveal about the path ahead.

02A / Macro Context

Inflation — The Fed's Dilemma

Headline CPI is manageable at 2.4%. But the Fed's preferred gauge — Core PCE — has re-accelerated to 3.06%, the widest divergence in this cycle.

CPI YoY 2.43%
Core PCE YoY 3.06%
Core PCE MoM (Jan) +0.36%
Annualized Run-Rate ~4.3%

Analysis

CPI YoY has been running in a narrow 2.3–2.5% range since mid-2025, giving a superficial impression that inflation is under control. But Core PCE — which strips out volatile food and energy and is the measure the Fed actually targets — tells a different story. It has re-accelerated from ~2.6% in mid-2025 to 3.06% in January 2026. The last two monthly prints (+0.36% each) annualize to approximately 4.3%, more than double the Fed's 2% target. The widening gap between headline CPI and Core PCE is a classic late-cycle warning: shelter, services, and sticky categories are driving prices higher even as goods disinflation helps the headline number.

02B / Macro Context

Labor Market — Softening But Not Breaking

Unemployment has drifted from 3.9% to 4.4% over two years. Payroll gains have flattened to near-zero. A slow grind, not a cliff — but the direction is clear.

Unemployment 4.4%
NFP Feb 2026 -92K
Peak UE 4.5% (Nov 2025)

Analysis

The labor market is not collapsing, which is precisely why the Fed feels it can wait. Unemployment rose from 3.9% in early 2024 to a peak of 4.5% in November 2025, before settling at 4.4% in February 2026. Nonfarm payroll growth has essentially stalled — several months in late 2025 and early 2026 show negative monthly changes, meaning the economy is no longer reliably adding jobs. The Sahm Rule recession indicator triggers when the 3-month moving average of unemployment rises 0.5pp above its 12-month low. With the low at 4.0% (Jan 2025) and the current rate at 4.4%, we are dangerously close. A single 4.6% print would trigger the rule.

02C / Macro Context

GDP Growth — The Growth Cliff

Quarterly GDP has been wildly erratic — from a contraction of -0.6% to a surge of 4.4% and back to 0.7%. The Committee walked into March with near-stall-speed growth.

Q4 2025 GDP 0.7%
Q3→Q4 Swing -3.7pp
2025 Full-Year Avg ~2.1%

Analysis

The GDP trajectory has been unusually volatile. Q1 2025 contracted at -0.6%, followed by a strong Q2-Q3 rebound (3.8% and 4.4%), only for Q4 to crash back to 0.7%. This boom-bust pattern — driven partly by inventory swings, trade dynamics, and fiscal impulses — makes it extremely difficult for the Committee to assess underlying trend growth. The 0.7% Q4 print was the freshest hard data the Committee had heading into the March meeting, and it paints a picture of an economy losing momentum fast. Note that GDP data lags by a full quarter — the Q1 2026 advance estimate won't arrive until late April.

03 / Policy Path

The Fed Funds Rate — From Cuts to Pause

175 basis points of cuts from September 2024 to December 2025. Then silence. The cutting cycle appears over — for now.

Analysis

The Fed began cutting in September 2024, taking the effective fed funds rate from 5.33% down to 3.64% by January 2026 — a cumulative 175bp of easing across 7 rate decisions. Then came the pause. January, February, and March 2026 all held steady at 3.64% (target range 3.50-3.75%). The March meeting confirmed that the pause is deliberate, not transitional. With Core PCE re-accelerating above 3%, the Committee cannot credibly justify further cuts without undermining its inflation mandate. The dissent from Miran (who wanted a 25bp cut) signals internal tension, but the 10-1 vote margin shows the majority firmly in the "wait" camp.

Key Takeaway

The Committee raised its 2026 PCE inflation median by 30bp (2.4% to 2.7%) and Core PCE by 20bp (2.5% to 2.7%), yet left the fed funds rate projections completely unchanged across all horizons. This implicitly signals the Committee is willing to tolerate higher inflation for longer rather than risk hiking back into a slowing economy. GDP projections were revised up modestly across all horizons, but this optimism conflicts with the actual Q4 2025 GDP print of 0.7% — the Committee appears to be projecting a rebound that has yet to materialize in the data. The longer-run neutral rate estimate ticked up from 3.0% to 3.1%, reflecting a growing consensus that the post-pandemic structural neutral rate is higher than pre-COVID levels.

04B / SEP Spotlight

The Dot Plot — A Divided Committee

For 2026, the dots split almost perfectly: 7 members see no more cuts, 7 see one more. The Committee has rarely been this evenly divided on near-term policy.

Dot Plot Analysis

The 2026 column is striking in its bimodality. Seven participants project the midpoint at 3.625% (the current level — meaning no more cuts this year), while seven project 3.375% (one additional 25bp cut). This near-perfect split reflects the fundamental disagreement: hawks see inflation as the binding constraint, doves see growth deceleration as the greater risk.

For 2027-2028, the distribution coalesces around 3.125% as the median — implying roughly 50bp of additional cuts over two years beyond 2026. But the range is wide: from 2.375% (aggressive dovish outlier) to 3.875% (a participant who sees rates staying near current levels through 2028).

The longer-run dots show significant dispersion (2.625% to 3.875%), with the median at 3.1% — up from 3.0% in December. This upward drift in the neutral rate estimate has major implications: it means the Committee believes the current stance may be less restrictive than previously thought, reducing the urgency to cut further.

Where the Marginal Information Is

The rate decision and dot plot median were entirely priced in — fed funds futures had assigned a 97% probability to a hold, and the median path was spot-on with consensus. This is not where the actionable signal lives.

The marginal information came in three layers:

1. The inflation-tolerance signal. Markets expected a 10-20bp PCE inflation upgrade. The actual +30bp — combined with zero change in the rate path — is a revealed preference statement. The Committee is explicitly choosing to run hotter for longer. For rates markets, this compresses the probability of near-term cuts and extends the duration of the current pause. The 2-year yield should reprice modestly higher as the market absorbs a wider inflation tolerance band.

2. The dot distribution, not the median. The median didn't move, but the shape changed dramatically. A 7-7 split on 2026 is far more divided than expected. This means every incoming data print carries outsized binary risk — one PCE report or one NFP miss could tip the balance from "hold" to "cut" (or vice versa). Options markets should reprice higher implied volatility around data releases.

3. The risk tilt as a leading indicator. The near-unanimous stagflation risk assessment (14/19 on GDP, 17/19 on inflation) is the strongest forward signal in the SEP. Historically, when the Committee's risk balance shifts this decisively, policy follows within 2-3 meetings. The Committee is pre-announcing its reaction function: if growth deteriorates further, they will cut despite elevated inflation. This is the put the market didn't fully have before this meeting.

06 / Conclusion

What This Means for Portfolios

The Fed is trapped. The data is noisy. The Committee is split. Here's how to think about positioning across horizons.

The Core Problem

The Fed faces a policy constraint that has no clean solution. Core PCE at 3.06% is too high to cut. GDP at 0.7% is too low to hike. Unemployment drifting toward 4.5% creates urgency to ease, but the inflation re-acceleration removes the permission to do so. The SEP reveals a Committee that upgraded inflation by 30bp and shrugged — choosing to absorb the overshoot rather than respond. This is a regime of deliberate inaction, and it has consequences for every asset class.

Short-Term Horizon (0–3 Months)

1.

The pause extends through May, likely June. With the dot plot split 7-7 and Core PCE above 3%, there is no coalition for a cut at the next meeting. The bar for action is a material growth shock (sub-zero GDP or a unemployment spike above 4.6%) or a convincing inflation deceleration (two consecutive Core PCE prints below 0.20% MoM). Neither is the base case in the near term.

2.

Every data print is now binary event risk. The 7-7 split means a single persuasive data point can tip the balance. The April Core PCE release (late May) is the highest-impact catalyst. If it prints below 0.25% MoM, the September cut reprices from ~40% to ~65%. If it prints above 0.30%, cut expectations get pushed to 2027. Position sizing should reflect this elevated data sensitivity.

3.

Carry trades remain attractive but fragile. The pause provides a stable rate floor that supports carry in front-end duration and short-vol strategies. But the tail risk is asymmetric — a growth shock triggers an aggressive dovish pivot (positive for duration, negative for vol sellers), while an inflation shock extends the pause (negative for risk). Hedge carry positions with OTM payer swaptions or VIX call spreads.

4.

Reduce beta, increase optionality. The stagflation risk tilt (14/19 GDP downside, 17/19 inflation upside) is not priced into equity implied vol at current levels. VIX at ~18-20 understates the realized volatility likely around data releases. Consider replacing directional equity exposure with long gamma positions through straddles on SPX and QQQ around CPI/PCE dates.

Medium-Term Horizon (3–12 Months)

1.

The reaction function has shifted — growth now dominates inflation. The risk balance is the clearest signal in the SEP. When 14 of 19 members flag downside GDP risk while raising inflation forecasts and holding rates, they are revealing their loss function: they fear recession more than persistent inflation. This means the next move is almost certainly a cut, not a hike — the question is timing, not direction. For medium-term positioning, this argues for overweighting duration vs. pre-meeting expectations.

2.

The neutral rate repricing is structural. The longer-run dot rising to 3.1% (from 2.5% pre-pandemic) means the market needs to reprice the entire terminal rate framework. Strategies that were built on a 2.5% neutral rate — long duration, growth equity, levered real estate — face permanent headwinds. The implication is a flatter yield curve at a higher level, compressing the equity risk premium. Favor cash-flow positive businesses over growth-at-any-price.

3.

Stagflation hedges deserve a portfolio allocation. The last time the Committee's risk balance looked like this was March 2022, when commodities and TIPS outperformed every other asset class over the subsequent 6 months. If the stagflation scenario materializes (inflation stays above 2.5% while GDP dips below 1% for two consecutive quarters), the optimal portfolio includes: long gold, long TIPS breakevens, short HY credit, and underweight small-cap equities. Even as a tail hedge at 5-10% of portfolio, this basket provides significant convexity.

4.

Crypto-specific: the correlation regime matters more than the level. BTC's correlation with equities has oscillated between 0.3 and 0.7 over the past year. In a stagflation scenario, this correlation typically compresses toward 0.7+ as both BTC and equities sell off on growth fears. The "digital gold" narrative only works when inflation is the primary driver — when growth collapses alongside inflation, BTC trades as a risk asset, not a store of value. Size positions accordingly: if your portfolio is already long equities, additional BTC exposure amplifies drawdown risk in the scenario the Committee itself is most worried about.

5.

Watch the Miran coalition. One dissent becomes three dissenters becomes a policy shift. If Q1 2026 GDP (reported late April) comes in negative — a second contraction in five quarters — the political and institutional pressure to cut will intensify regardless of inflation. The Committee's own risk assessment has already teed this up. A dovish pivot would be a high-velocity catalyst for risk assets across the board: long equities, long credit, long BTC, short DXY. The setup is there; the trigger is the data.
