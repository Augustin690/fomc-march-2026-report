01 / Overview

Executive Summary

The Federal Open Market Committee voted 10-1 on March 18, 2026 to hold the federal funds rate at 3.50–3.75%, marking the third consecutive meeting without a rate change. Governor Stephen Miran cast the lone dissent, preferring a 25bp cut.

The statement acknowledged that "economic activity has been expanding at a solid pace" but noted that "job gains have remained low" and "inflation remains somewhat elevated." For the first time in this cycle, the Committee flagged "developments in the Middle East" as a source of elevated uncertainty.

The macro backdrop tells a more troubling story than the statement's measured language suggests. Core PCE has re-accelerated to 3.06% YoY, GDP growth collapsed to 0.7% in Q4 2025, and the Committee's own risk assessments reveal near-unanimous stagflation anxiety. This report examines the data the Committee faced and what their projections reveal about the path ahead.

02A / Macro Context

Inflation — The Fed's Dilemma

Headline CPI is manageable at 2.4%. But the Fed's preferred gauge — Core PCE — has re-accelerated to 3.06%, the widest divergence in this cycle.

Core PCE YoY 3.06%

Annualized Run-Rate ~4.3%

Analysis

CPI YoY has been running in a narrow 2.3–2.5% range since mid-2025, giving a superficial impression that inflation is under control. But Core PCE — which strips out volatile food and energy and is the measure the Fed actually targets — tells a different story. It has re-accelerated from ~2.6% in mid-2025 to 3.06% in January 2026. The last two monthly prints (+0.36% each) annualize to approximately 4.3%, more than double the Fed's 2% target. The widening gap between headline CPI and Core PCE is a classic late-cycle warning: shelter, services, and sticky categories are driving prices higher even as goods disinflation helps the headline number.

02B / Macro Context

Labor Market — Softening But Not Breaking

Unemployment has drifted from 3.9% to 4.4% over two years. Payroll gains have flattened to near-zero. A slow grind, not a cliff — but the direction is clear.

Unemployment 4.4%
NFP Feb 2026 -92K
Peak UE 4.5% (Nov 2025)

Analysis

The labor market is not collapsing, which is precisely why the Fed feels it can wait. Unemployment rose from 3.9% in early 2024 to a peak of 4.5% in November 2025, before settling at 4.4% in February 2026. Nonfarm payroll growth has essentially stalled — several months in late 2025 and early 2026 show negative monthly changes, meaning the economy is no longer reliably adding jobs. The Sahm Rule recession indicator triggers when the 3-month moving average of unemployment rises 0.5pp above its 12-month low. With the low at 4.0% (Jan 2025) and the current rate at 4.4%, we are dangerously close. A single 4.6% print would trigger the rule.

02C / Macro Context

GDP Growth — The Growth Cliff

Quarterly GDP has been wildly erratic — from a contraction of -0.6% to a surge of 4.4% and back to 0.7%. The Committee walked into March with near-stall-speed growth.

Q4 2025 GDP 0.7%
Q3→Q4 Swing -3.7pp
2025 Full-Year Avg ~2.1%

Analysis

The GDP trajectory has been unusually volatile. Q1 2025 contracted at -0.6%, followed by a strong Q2-Q3 rebound (3.8% and 4.4%), only for Q4 to crash back to 0.7%. This boom-bust pattern — driven partly by inventory swings, trade dynamics, and fiscal impulses — makes it extremely difficult for the Committee to assess underlying trend growth. The 0.7% Q4 print was the freshest hard data the Committee had heading into the March meeting, and it paints a picture of an economy losing momentum fast. Note that GDP data lags by a full quarter — the Q1 2026 advance estimate won't arrive until late April.

03 / Policy Path

The Fed Funds Rate — From Cuts to Pause

175 basis points of cuts from September 2024 to December 2025. Then silence. The cutting cycle appears over — for now.

Analysis

The Fed began cutting in September 2024, taking the effective fed funds rate from 5.33% down to 3.64% by January 2026 — a cumulative 175bp of easing across 7 rate decisions. Then came the pause. January, February, and March 2026 all held steady at 3.64% (target range 3.50-3.75%). The March meeting confirmed that the pause is deliberate, not transitional. With Core PCE re-accelerating above 3%, the Committee cannot credibly justify further cuts without undermining its inflation mandate. The dissent from Miran (who wanted a 25bp cut) signals internal tension, but the 10-1 vote margin shows the majority firmly in the "wait" camp.

Key Takeaway

The headline story is the inflation upgrade with no change in the rate path. The Committee raised its 2026 PCE inflation median by 30bp (2.4% to 2.7%) and Core PCE by 20bp (2.5% to 2.7%), yet left the fed funds rate projections completely unchanged across all horizons. This implicitly signals the Committee is willing to tolerate higher inflation for longer rather than risk hiking back into a slowing economy. GDP projections were revised up modestly across all horizons, but this optimism conflicts with the actual Q4 2025 GDP print of 0.7% — the Committee appears to be projecting a rebound that has yet to materialize in the data. The longer-run neutral rate estimate ticked up from 3.0% to 3.1%, reflecting a growing consensus that the post-pandemic structural neutral rate is higher than pre-COVID levels.

04B / SEP Spotlight

The Dot Plot — A Divided Committee

For 2026, the dots split almost perfectly: 7 members see no more cuts, 7 see one more. The Committee has rarely been this evenly divided on near-term policy.

Dot Plot Analysis

The 2026 column is striking in its bimodality. Seven participants project the midpoint at 3.625% (the current level — meaning no more cuts this year), while seven project 3.375% (one additional 25bp cut). This near-perfect split reflects the fundamental disagreement: hawks see inflation as the binding constraint, doves see growth deceleration as the greater risk.

For 2027-2028, the distribution coalesces around 3.125% as the median — implying roughly 50bp of additional cuts over two years beyond 2026. But the range is wide: from 2.375% (aggressive dovish outlier) to 3.875% (a participant who sees rates staying near current levels through 2028).

The longer-run dots show significant dispersion (2.625% to 3.875%), with the median at 3.1% — up from 3.0% in December. This upward drift in the neutral rate estimate has major implications: it means the Committee believes the current stance may be less restrictive than previously thought, reducing the urgency to cut further.

14 of 19 participants see GDP growth risks tilted to the downside. 16-17 of 19 see inflation and unemployment risks tilted to the upside. The uncertainty diffusion index for March 2026 stands at 0.79 for GDP, 0.84 for unemployment, and 0.84 for PCE inflation — all elevated by historical standards. The last time all four indicators were simultaneously this high was March 2025, during the initial tariff shock. The Fed is telling markets, in its own institutional language, that visibility is exceptionally poor and the worst-case scenario involves simultaneous deterioration on both sides of the dual mandate.

05 / Conclusion

What This Means

1.

The Fed is trapped between sticky inflation (Core PCE 3.06%) and slowing growth (GDP 0.7%). Neither side of the dual mandate permits decisive action.

2.

The SEP shows the Committee upgraded inflation forecasts by 20-30bp but left the rate path unchanged — they are choosing to wait and hope rather than act preemptively in either direction.

3.

The dot plot is split almost exactly down the middle on whether another cut happens in 2026. This binary outcome creates event risk around every future meeting and data release.

4.

The risk balance is the most stagflationary since March 2022 — near-unanimous downside GDP risk combined with upside inflation risk. The Committee itself is worried about stagflation.

5.

Miran's dissent (wanting a cut) establishes a formal dovish anchor. If growth deteriorates further, more members could join the cut camp. The threshold for action is lower than the 10-1 vote suggests.

6.

For markets: the pause is likely to continue through at least the May meeting. The next Core PCE print is the single most important data point — if it stays above 3%, cut expectations get pushed further out. If GDP collapses below zero again in Q1 2026, the pressure to cut will intensify regardless of inflation.
